Emmy Noether

Mathematics

Notable Achievements

  • Made formative contributions to the field of abstract algebra
  • Developed the Noether Theory which connects mathematical symmetry to the conservation of energy
  • Received Ackermann-Teubner Memorial Award for contributions to mathematics

Amalie Emmy Noether was born in Germany in 1882. She audited classes in mathematics at the University of Erlangen because women were not allowed to be full students there. Once they allowed women to study there officially, she received her PhD and worked upaid on her mathematical research and as a teaching assistant and lecturer to her father.

In 1915, she was invited to work with mathematicians David Hilbert and Felix Klein on the math behind Einstein's theory of relativity. She also began teaching at the University of GÃ¶ttingen, where this research was being done. Here, Noether discovered that if that Lagrangian of a system does not change when the coordinate system changes, then there is some quantity conserved. For example, if the Lagrangian does not change when time is changed, then energy must be conserved. This has become known as Noether's theorum and is an important result in theoretical physics.

In addition to the Noether theorum, Emmy Noether made significant contributions in the fields of noncommutative algebras, as well as theories of rings and fields. She is also credited as a contributor in several lines of research by other mathematicians, including fields like algebraic topology.

Emmy Noether left Germany in 1933 to escape the Nazis, and went to the United States to teach at Bryn Mawr College and conduct research in New Jersey. However, she died of cancer in 1935. After her death, she was described as "the most significant mathemetical genius thus far produced since the higher education of women began."
